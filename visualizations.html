Data Science 4200 Final Project Visualizations

First Chart

The heatmap shows differences in institutional trust across political party groups. Because lower values indicate greater trust, the darker red cells represent institutions that are viewed more favorably within a given party. The chart shows that trust is not distributed evenly across institutions or across political groups. Some institutions have more trust from all groups while others have differences between them. By displaying all party groups and institutions at once, the heatmap makes it easy to spot which organizations are broadly trusted, which are more polarizing, and where Republicans, Democrats, Independents, and non affiliated respondents diverge most in their views.

Second Chart

Across all party affiliations, voting consistently ranks as one of the most important civic activities, with Republicans scoring it the highest of any group on any question. Republicans also stand out in their strong emphasis on believing in God, displaying the flag, and supporting the military, rating these notably higher than Democrats and Independents. Democrats and Independents tend to score most activities similarly, with the notable exception of protesting, where Democrats rate it considerably more important than any other group. The "None" (no party preference) group shows the most variation across questions, spiking unusually high on believing in God and displaying the flag while scoring low on most other civic activities. Across all parties, knowing the Pledge of Allegiance and serving on a jury are rated among the least important activities, suggesting broad agreement on which civic behaviors feel less essential regardless of political affiliation.

Third Chart

The pie chart focuses on respondents of the survey who reported that they always vote and shows how this group is distributed across political groups. By using the gender and income dropdown filters, viewers can see whether the party composition of these voters shifts across the party subgroups. In the default view, the chart gives us an overall understanding of which political affiliations make up the largest shares of the always voters, while the filtered views help reveal whether certain income levels or gender groups are more heavily represented within particular parties. This allows the chart to highlight how consistent voting behavior intersects with both demographic background and party identity.

Fourth Chart

The bubble chart reveals a consistent pattern across all party affiliations: the majority of respondents — regardless of party — who find voting "very easy" are also the most frequent voters. Republicans and Democrats show similar overall turnout distributions, with both parties clustering their largest bubbles at "very easy" for "always" and "sporadic" voters. Independents follow the same trend but with smaller group sizes. Notably, even among respondents who find voting "somewhat" or "very difficult," a significant portion still report always voting, suggesting that perceived barriers alone do not fully deter committed voters. The smallest and most scattered bubbles appear in the "rarely/never" row across all difficulty levels, indicating that infrequent voters are a minority within every party and every perceived ease category.

Fifth Chart

The interactive stacked bar chart shows that respondents across all political parties generally view many civic activities as important, but the strength of that importance varies by party depending on the type of activity selected. For example, some activities show stronger responses across all the categories, while others show a stronger response in certain groups. Republicans, Democrats, and Independents often share similar overall response structures, but the proportions within each category are different depending on the civic duty chosen. Because each bar is normalized to 100%, the chart makes it easy to compare response distributions across parties.
