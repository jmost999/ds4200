Data Science 4200 Final Project Visualizations

How Parties Differ in the Importance They Place on Civic Activities

Party Breakdown of Consistent Voters

This chart helps highlight that among respondents who report always voting, the largest share identify as Democrats, followed by Republicans and Independents, while only a much smaller portion identify as Other or None. It shows that consistent voting behavior in this dataset is concentrated most heavily among respondents aligned with the major political parties. The gender and income filters help reveal whether this pattern remains similar across demographic groups or whether the balance of party identification shifts within certain subpopulations. Overall, the chart highlights how regular voting behavior is distributed across political affiliation and helps show which groups make up the core of the always voting population.

How Political Party Shapes Views of Civic Duty by Importance Score

This box and whisker plot helps highlight that views on civic duty are not the same depending on the political party a person identifies with in this dataset. Here, civic duties are compared using an importance score, where lower scores represent higher importance, which makes it easier to see how each party tends to rate these responsibilities overall while also showing the spread in responses. While all groups rate some duties as more important than others, the filtered views suggest that Democrats and Independents tend to rate protesting as more important than Republicans do. Republicans appear to place more importance on displaying a flag and supporting the military, while Democrats rate knowing the Pledge and believing in God differently from Republicans, with Republicans seeming to place more importance on believing in God. The chart also shows that some duties, like voting, are rated as important across groups, while others vary more depending on party identity. Overall, the visual helps tell the story that ideas about what makes a good citizen shift across political groups in the dataset.

How does Each Party View Ease of Voting To Be An Issue Which Influences Voter Turnout?

This heatmap helps show that in the dataset, people who say voting is very easy or somewhat easy tend to appear more often among those who vote always or sporadically, especially in the larger party groups like Democrats and Republicans. The dropdown adds another layer to the story by letting the viewer compare this pattern across parties. For example, Democrats and Republicans both show their largest counts among respondents who see voting as very easy, while smaller groups like Other and None still follow a similar general pattern, though with fewer respondents overall. The chart also shows that some people still report voting regularly even when they see voting as somewhat or very difficult, which suggests that difficulty may matter, but it does not fully stop participation. Overall, the visual highlights a relationship in the dataset between perceived ease of voting, voting frequency, and party affiliation.

Institutional Trust Across Political Parties

The heatmap shows differences in institutional trust across political party groups. Because lower values indicate greater trust, the darker red cells represent institutions that are viewed more favorably within a given party. The chart shows that trust is not distributed evenly across institutions or across political groups. Some institutions have more trust from all groups while others have differences between them. By displaying all party groups and institutions at once, the heatmap makes it easy to spot which organizations are broadly trusted, which are more polarizing, and where Republicans, Democrats, Independents, and non affiliated respondents diverge most in their views.
