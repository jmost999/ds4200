Second Chart

Across all party affiliations, voting consistently ranks as one of the most important civic activities, with Republicans scoring it the highest of any group on any question. Republicans also stand out in their strong emphasis on believing in God, displaying the flag, and supporting the military, rating these notably higher than Democrats and Independents. Democrats and Independents tend to score most activities similarly, with the notable exception of protesting, where Democrats rate it considerably more important than any other group. The "None" (no party preference) group shows the most variation across questions, spiking unusually high on believing in God and displaying the flag while scoring low on most other civic activities. Across all parties, knowing the Pledge of Allegiance and serving on a jury are rated among the least important activities, suggesting broad agreement on which civic behaviors feel less essential regardless of political affiliation.

Fourth Chart

The bubble chart reveals a consistent pattern across all party affiliations: the majority of respondents — regardless of party — who find voting "very easy" are also the most frequent voters. Republicans and Democrats show similar overall turnout distributions, with both parties clustering their largest bubbles at "very easy" for "always" and "sporadic" voters. Independents follow the same trend but with smaller group sizes. Notably, even among respondents who find voting "somewhat" or "very difficult," a significant portion still report always voting, suggesting that perceived barriers alone do not fully deter committed voters. The smallest and most scattered bubbles appear in the "rarely/never" row across all difficulty levels, indicating that infrequent voters are a minority within every party and every perceived ease category.
